Sea Ice Coverage in the Hudson Bay Region

Geospatial analysis of Arctic sea ice dynamics based on daily satellite observations.

Scientific Context

Sea ice coverage is a key indicator of climate change in polar regions. The Hudson Bay area is particularly sensitive due to its relatively shallow depth and strong seasonal variability.

This project analyzes long-term changes in ice coverage by combining daily satellite observations with geospatial processing techniques. The focus lies on quantifying:

  • Seasonal freezing and melting cycles
  • Interannual variability
  • Long-term trends in ice extent

The region is defined using a geographic mask derived from coastline data. Only ocean grid cells are included in the analysis to ensure a physically meaningful normalization of ice coverage.

Data Pipeline

The analysis is based on daily sea ice concentration data spanning multiple decades. Raw data is processed in several steps:

  1. Download daily satellite-derived sea ice concentration fields
  2. Extract the Hudson Bay region using a geographic mask
  3. Compute total ice-covered area relative to ocean surface
  4. Aggregate results into time series

Due to the large volume of input data (daily resolution over ~40 years), preprocessing is computationally expensive and is therefore separated from the visualization layer.

The time series highlights both seasonal cycles and long-term trends. Recent decades show a clear tendency towards earlier melting and delayed freezing.

The polar representation emphasizes structural changes in the seasonal cycle, revealing an expansion of ice-free periods and increased variability.

Outlook

The current implementation focuses on a fixed region. Future extensions include:

  • Interactive region selection (Region Picker)
  • Support for multiple datasets and satellite products
  • Automated generation of animations and seasonal summaries
